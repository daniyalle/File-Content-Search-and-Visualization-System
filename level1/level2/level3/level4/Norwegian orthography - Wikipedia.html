Norwegian orthography is the method of writing the Norwegian language, of which there are two written standards: Bokml and Nynorsk. While Bokml has for the most part derived its forms from the written Danish language and Danish-Norwegian speech, Nynorsk gets its word forms from Aasen's reconstructed "base dialect", which is intended to represent the distinctive dialectal forms. Both standards use a 29-letter variant of the Latin alphabet and the same orthographic principles.

Alphabet[edit]

The Norwegian alphabet is based upon the Latin alphabet and is identical to the Danish alphabet. Since 1917 it has consisted of the following 29 letters.

Diacritics[edit]

Norwegian (especially the Nynorsk variant) also uses several letters with diacritic signs: , , , , , , and . The diacritic signs are not compulsory,[1] but can be added to clarify the meaning of words (homonyms) which otherwise would be identical. One example is ein gut ("a boy") versus in gut ("one boy"), in Nynorsk, and en gutt ("a boy") versus n gutt ("one boy") in Bokml. Diacritics are obligatory in foreign proper names that use them, like Rhne, Lige, Linn, Mnchen. In other loanwords diacritics are optional, like crme frache, tte--tte. If the loanword has been adapted for Norwegian use, diacritics that were there originally should not be included, as in ampere, bohem, opera.[1] The Norwegian vowels , and never take diacritics.

The diacritic signs in use include the acute accent, grave accent and the circumflex. A common example of how the diacritics change the meaning of a word, is for:

  • for (preposition. For or to, Bokml and Nynorsk)
  • fr (verb. Went, in the sense went quickly, Bokml and Nynorsk.)
  • fr (noun. Furrow, only Nynorsk)
  • fr (noun. Fodder, feed, fodder, Bokml and Nynorsk)

can be used in g, meaning "also". This word is found in both Nynorsk and Bokml. An example of in Nynorsk is the word vr, meaning "weather".

History[edit]

The letter (HTML &aring;) was officially introduced in Norwegian in 1917, replacing Aa or aa. The new letter came from the Swedish alphabet, where it had been in official use since the 18th century. The former digraph Aa still occurs in personal names. Geographical names tend to follow the current orthography, meaning that the letter will be used. Family names may not follow modern orthography, and as such retain the digraph aa where would be used today. Aa remains in use as a transliteration, if the letter is not available for technical reasons. Aa is treated like in alphabetical sorting, not like two adjacent letters A, meaning that while a is the first letter of the alphabet, aa is the last. This rule does not apply to non-Scandinavian names, so a modern atlas would list the German city of Aachen under A but list the Danish city of Aabenraa under .

A difference between the Dano-Norwegian and the Swedish alphabet is that Swedish uses the variant instead of , and the variant instead of (like German). Also, the collating order for these three letters is different: , , .

  • NS 4551-1, later established in international standard ISO 646
